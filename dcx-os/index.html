Section 01b / Future of B2B Customer Service

From ticket queue
to resolution engine

Traditional service was organized around channels: phone, email, chat, case queues. The future model is organized around intent and resolution. The goal is to solve the customer's need before it becomes a case. Self-service is the first line of support. AI handles routine resolution. Humans step in where judgment, empathy, and commercial nuance matter most.

The new operating model

01
Digital Self-Service
Portals, help centers, guided workflows, AI search, and answer engines reduce avoidable contacts. The best resolution is the one the customer never has to ask for.
02
AI Resolution
Agents handle routine requests, triage, summarize, route, and execute simple tasks across systems. They identify intent, retrieve knowledge, complete workflows, and escalate cleanly when confidence is low.
03
Human-in-the-Loop
Specialists handle exceptions, high-value customers, escalations, commercial nuance, and relationship moments. A single conversation history ensures continuity so the customer never repeats themselves.
04
Customer Growth
Success, adoption, renewal, and expansion teams work closely with service because retention and experience are now linked. The org behaves like an experience and revenue system, not a cost center with a queue.

How roles evolve

Before
Traditional Service Org
Tier-1 Support Agent
Answers repetitive questions from a script. Measured on cases closed per hour and handle time.
Queue Manager
Manages case routing, staffing levels, and SLA adherence. Focus is throughput and coverage.
Call Center Supervisor
Monitors call quality, handles escalations, manages attendance and scheduling.
Support Admin
Configures case forms, manages assignment rules, runs reports on case volumes and backlogs.
Knowledge Base Owner
Writes and updates help articles. Measured on article count and coverage.
After
AI-Enabled Resolution Org
Resolution Specialist
Handles exceptions, account-aware escalations, and relationship-sensitive moments. Measured on customer outcome and resolution quality, not volume.
Orchestration Lead
Designs routing logic, containment strategy, and human/AI handoff thresholds. Owns resolution rate and deflection metrics.
AI + Human Performance Coach
Coaches agents on AI-assisted workflows, monitors AI quality, and optimizes the human-AI collaboration model.
Automation & Workflow Designer
Maps journeys, defines handoffs, builds resolution paths across systems. Manages bot behavior, workflow logic, and escalation rules.
Knowledge Engineer
Curates content for both AI retrieval and human use. Optimizes answer structures, retrieval quality, and knowledge freshness so AI resolution rates improve continuously.

Emerging roles

Conversation Designer
New Role
Designs chat and AI interactions so they feel natural, useful, and safe. Bridges UX writing, dialog design, and AI prompt engineering.
AI Operations Manager
New Role
Monitors resolution quality, containment rates, handoff accuracy, and model behavior. Owns the AI agent performance loop.
CX Operations Lead
New Role
Connects service, digital, product, and analytics into one improvement loop. Translates CX signals into operational changes.
Customer Insights Analyst
New Role
Turns service and feedback data into trends, root causes, and action plans. Feeds insight back into product, content, and support design.
Customer Success Digital Specialist
Evolved Role
Works on onboarding, adoption, and proactive intervention through digital channels. Blends success management with digital execution.
Digital Experience Manager
Evolved Role
Owns self-service portal UX, knowledge discoverability, and digital deflection strategy. Measures experience by friction removed, not pages published.

Skills to level up

01
AI Literacy
Understanding what AI can and cannot do, how to supervise it, and how to evaluate AI resolution quality. Every service leader needs this.
02
Data Fluency
Reading CX metrics, containment rates, journey friction, and account health signals. Moving from gut-feel to data-informed decision making.
03
Emotional Intelligence
As AI handles routine work, humans focus more on trust, empathy, and difficult conversations. The bar for human interaction quality goes up.
04
Workflow Design
Mapping journeys, defining handoffs, and building resolution paths across systems. Thinking in workflows, not tickets.
05
Knowledge Architecture
Content governance, retrieval design, and answer quality optimization. If AI can't find the right answer, containment breaks down.
06
Cross-Functional Leadership
Service no longer operates in a silo. Leaders must partner across product, digital, commerce, and data teams to close the experience loop.
What good looks like
A mature future-state org deflects simple questions through self-service and AI, routes complex interactions to the right human quickly, uses customer data to personalize help and reduce repetition, and closes the loop from support back into product, content, and commerce. It measures CX not by tickets closed, but by friction removed and revenue protected. The winning organization feels easy to the customer because it makes the right thing the easy thing: self-serve when possible, human help when necessary, and seamless escalation when it matters.
Section 02 / The Customer Journey

From discovery to expansion.
One continuous loop.

Customers do not think in CRM, ERP, or service clouds. They think: "solve my problem with minimal friction." Here's what the journey looks like in practice: what they want, what enables it, and the signals it generates.

Stage 01 / Pre-Buy

Discover and
Evaluate

The buyer is researching independently, increasingly through AI answer engines before ever touching a website. They're building scientific or business confidence, validating fit, and quietly assembling a buying committee that may be invisible to your CRM.

Customer Goals
  • Learn, compare, validate quickly
  • Build internal confidence and business case
  • Estimate cost without sales pressure
  • Configure compatibility for their workflow
Signals Generated
  • Content engagement, search terms, intent
  • Anonymous visitor identity, account match
  • Buying committee mapping, stakeholder signals
  • Quote velocity, product affinity
Stage 02 / Buy

Configure and
Transact

The moment of transaction is increasingly digital, increasingly automated, and increasingly happening inside the buyer's own procurement system. Friction here costs revenue directly. The winning experience meets buyers where they are: punchout, EDI, marketplaces, portal, or whatever combination keeps them out of email.

Customer Goals
  • Frictionless ordering and reorder
  • Contract pricing, approvals, transparency
  • Procurement-system integration (punchout, EDI)
  • Configurable quotes for complex deals
Signals Generated
  • Order patterns, pricing elasticity
  • Procurement friction, abandonment points
  • Stakeholder approval velocity
  • Channel attribution and revenue influence
Stage 03 / Post-Buy

Use, Resolve,
Expand

This is where most enterprises lose the customer. The mature model isn't "handle cases faster." It's prevent cases entirely. Autonomous self-serve resolves what it can, AI-assisted chat handles the next tier, humans engage only when judgment, nuance, or revenue is at stake.

Customer Goals
  • Fast time-to-value, easy onboarding
  • Reliability, productivity, minimal downtime
  • Fast answers without repeating themselves
  • Predictive guidance, not reactive support
Signals Generated
  • Adoption progress, feature utilization
  • Health scores, support risk, churn signals
  • Expansion opportunities, cross-sell intent
  • Knowledge gaps, deflection failure points
Section 03 / Common Architecture

The four-layer
operating model.

Every customer interaction generates a signal. Those signals flow through orchestration into data and AI, where they sharpen a model or trigger an action. The output flows back as a more personalized moment, which generates the next signal. Every mature DCX program, regardless of vendor mix, converges on the same four-layer pattern to make this loop faster, smarter, and more autonomous.

Layer 01 / Top Experience
The surfaces customers touch. Channels are no longer the strategy. Omnichannel continuity is. Context must follow the customer everywhere so they never start over.
Web · Portal · Mobile · Marketplace · Punchout · EDI · Field Sales · Contact Center · Service Tech · Distributors · AI Assistants · Conversational UI
Layer 02 / Middle Orchestration
The hidden engine. Coordinates journeys, triggers actions, routes work, personalizes interactions, decides next-best-actions. Increasingly agentic. This is where enterprises differentiate in 2026.
Journey Orchestration · Workflow Engines · Event Streaming · Decision Engines · Agentic AI · Business Rules · Customer Journey Intelligence · Next-Best-Action
Layer 03 / Brain Data and AI
Unified customer understanding. The golden record from MDM, behavioral signals from CDP, predictive models, knowledge graphs, and AI agents. All sitting on a governed foundation that powers personalization, prioritization, and automation.
MDM · CDP · Data Warehouse / Lakehouse · Feature Stores · Vector DB · Identity Resolution · Knowledge Graphs · LLM Orchestration · Consent / Governance
Layer 04 / Body Execution
Systems of record and execution. Where operational reality meets the customer promise. ERP, CRM, supply chain, pricing, billing. When these are wrong, the experience breaks no matter how good the front end looks.
ERP · CRM · Supply Chain · Pricing · Inventory · Manufacturing · Service Systems · Logistics · Billing · Procurement · Order Management

Section 05 / The Three Enablers

How AI, data, and digital
build the experience of the future.

These three are not separate strategies. They are a stack. Digital is the surface, data is the substrate, AI is the multiplier. Each one is dead without the other two.

◐

Digital Strategy

The visible expression of the operating model. Surfaces, channels, and journeys designed around customer intent rather than internal org charts. The layer that meets buyers where they are.

  • Procurement-native commerce (punchout, EDI, marketplaces)
  • Omnichannel continuity and context portability
  • Self-service portals with entitlement awareness
  • Conversational interfaces and AI search optimization
  • Composable architecture where flexibility actually pays off
◇

Data Strategy

The unified foundation that makes everything else possible. Without it, AI fragments, personalization fails, and digital becomes a stack of disconnected point solutions. Governance becomes a feature, not a constraint.

  • Golden record via MDM (Reltio, Informatica, Tamr, Semarchy)
  • Real-time activation via CDP (Salesforce Data 360, Adobe, Hightouch)
  • Identity resolution across anonymous and known signals
  • Consent, privacy, and AI guardrails as first-class infrastructure
  • Multi-touch attribution connecting touchpoint to revenue
✦

AI Strategy

The intelligence and orchestration layer that turns data into decisions and decisions into action. The 2026 shift is from AI-as-assistant to AI-as-actor. Agents that complete tasks, not just suggest them.

  • Agentic resolution (Agentforce, Fin, Ada, Decagon, Forethought)
  • AI-powered search and recommendations (Coveo, Algolia, Bloomreach)
  • Predictive lead scoring, propensity, next-best-action
  • Content generation, translation, enrichment at scale
  • Embedded copilots across CRM, commerce, service, success
Section 05b / The Measurement Framework

Measuring what actually matters
across the full journey.

Most organizations measure activity. Campaigns sent. Tickets closed. Calls handled. The mature DCX organization measures outcomes, prevention, and compounding value. The difference shows up in every stage of the customer journey, not just in the service center.

The framework below maps traditional KPIs against their DCX-mature equivalents across six journey dimensions. This is more than a cost-to-serve shift. It is a complete rethinking of what you optimize for, what you hold teams accountable to, and what signals you feed back into the operating model.

The core reframe

From handling cases to preventing them. From measuring activity to measuring the absence of friction.

Traditional KPIs optimize the cost of work that already happened. Mature DCX KPIs optimize for work that never needs to happen. A prevented case, a self-served reorder, a proactive alert that stops a churn signal before it becomes a conversation. The best customer experience is the one the customer never has to ask for.

Section 06 / The Maturity Curve

DCX maturity.

Not every organization is mature, and that's fine. The trap is mistaking what stage you're actually at. Most enterprises self-report two stages higher than they are. Here's the honest progression.

01
Fragmented
Channel chaos
  • Siloed systems per OpCo / BU
  • Channel inconsistency
  • Reactive support model
  • Limited analytics, gut decisions
  • Manual processes everywhere
02
Connected
Integration phase
  • Channels integrated, data shared
  • Standardized workflows emerging
  • Self-service introduced
  • Common customer record forming
  • Basic KPIs, dashboard culture
03
Intelligent
Insight-driven
  • AI recommendations operational
  • Predictive insights at scale
  • Journey orchestration live
  • Personalization beyond segments
  • Digital adoption optimized
04
Proactive
Prevention model
  • Issues predicted and prevented
  • Dynamic workflows adapt to signal
  • AI-assisted operations everywhere

Three-tier practical view

What this actually looks like in practice.

The five-stage model is useful for strategy. For execution, most teams need three answers: where am I today, where should I be in 18 months, and what does mature really mean?

Tier / Beginner

The Fragmented
Enterprise

Maturity Stage 1–2 · Most mid-market and conglomerate B2B

What you'll see

  • Multiple CRMs across BUs, no shared customer record
  • Channel-by-channel marketing, no orchestration
  • Self-service is a static FAQ page
  • Attribution is last-touch, mostly wrong
  • AI is a chatbot the team is embarrassed about

Tech stack signature

  • CRM (one or many), basic email tool
  • WordPress, Sitecore, or homegrown CMS
  • Google Analytics, exported to spreadsheets
  • Zendesk or basic ticketing
  • No CDP, no MDM, no journey orchestration

Org reality

  • Marketing and service report to different leaders
  • Digital is a function inside Marketing
  • Data team is in IT, not Commercial
  • AI strategy is "we're looking into it"
Tier / Intermediate

The Connected
Enterprise

Maturity Stage 2–3 · Where most serious B2B programs sit

What you'll see

  • Unified golden record across CRM, commerce, service
  • Multi-touch attribution informing investment
  • Marketing automation tied to lifecycle stages
  • Self-service deflects 30–50% of basic cases
  • Personalization on top accounts, segments broadly

Tech stack signature

  • Salesforce CRM plus CDP (Data 360) or Adobe stack
  • MDM in place (Reltio, Informatica, Tamr)
  • Snowflake or Databricks as data backbone
  • Marketo, AEM/PIM, Dreamdata or Marketo Measure
  • Agentforce, Fin, or Forethought piloted in support

Org reality

  • Chief Digital or Chief Customer Officer role exists
  • Cross-functional Commercial Ops or CxC team formed
  • AI governance council assembled, ethics frameworks live
  • Data team partnered with Commercial, not just IT
Tier / Mature

The Autonomous
Enterprise

Maturity Stage 4–5 · The reference cases, rare in B2B today

What you'll see

  • Agentic AI handles 60%+ of inbound resolution autonomously
  • Personalization is real-time, account-aware, contract-aware
  • Procurement integration (punchout, EDI) is core, not edge
  • Predictive churn and expansion drive Customer Success
  • Cost-to-serve cut 30–50% while CSAT rises

  • Composable commerce plus CPQ for complex deals

  • Center of Excellence with embedded pods in BUs
  • Engineering, data, and commercial fused under one P&L
  • Continuous experimentation as default operating mode

    Section 08 / Org Design

    Three common org
    configurations    .

    The technology is the easy part. The hard part is where the DCX function lives, who it reports to, and how it relates to BUs and IT. Here are the three configurations that work in B2B today, each with real trade-offs.

    01
    Configuration / Centralized

    The Center of
    Excellence

    One central DCX team, owned by a Chief Digital Officer, Chief Customer Officer, or Chief AI Officer, sets standards, owns shared platforms, and partners with BUs through a federated delivery model. Common pattern in multi-OpCo life sciences and diagnostics enterprises.

    Reports to
    CDO / CCO / CAIO, usually CEO direct report
    Owns
    Platforms, standards, data, AI, analytics
    BU role
    Adopt and localize, fund via shared services or chargeback
    Best for
    Multi-OpCo conglomerates, diversified portfolios
    Strengths
    • Platform standardization and cost leverage
    • Enterprise data and AI consistency
    • Talent concentration
    • Strong governance
    Watch-outs
    • BU resistance, "not invented here"
    • Slow when BU needs are unique
    • Demand outpaces central capacity
    • Funding tensions
    02
    Configuration / Federated

    The Hub and
    Spoke

    A small central hub sets standards and owns enterprise platforms, but most digital, AI, and CX work lives inside the business units. The hub is a force multiplier, not a delivery organization. Common in operating companies with strong P&L autonomy.

    Reports to
    CDO or COO at corporate; BU spokes report to BU leaders
    Owns
    Reference architecture, shared data, AI governance
    BU role
    Execute, fund, customize within enterprise guardrails
    Best for
    Federated operating models, decentralized P&Ls
    Strengths
    • BU ownership and speed
    • Customer proximity preserved
    • Enterprise leverage where it matters
    • Lower central headcount
    Watch-outs
    • Standards drift over time
    • Talent stratification across BUs
    • Data fragmentation if hub is too thin
    • Coordination overhead
    03
    Configuration / Embedded

    The Customer
    Experience Org

    DCX is fused into a single Customer Experience organization that owns the entire journey end-to-end: acquisition, commerce, service, and success. Often led by a Chief Customer Officer with direct P&L accountability. The most ambitious, and hardest to execute.

    Reports to
    CCO direct report to CEO, peer to CRO and CFO
    Owns
    Full journey: marketing, commerce, service, success, data
    BU role
    Product or market focus; CX layer is enterprise-wide
    Best for
    Single-portfolio enterprises, customer-led strategies
    Strengths
    • End-to-end journey ownership
    • Strongest customer outcomes
    • Highest AI and data leverage
    • Clear accountability for NPS and retention
    Watch-outs
    • Massive change management
    • Threatens traditional sales and marketing power
    • Requires executive air cover
    • Rare to see fully realized
