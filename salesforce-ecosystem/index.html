A neutral, practitioner-first map of the Salesforce platform: what they bought, what it is called now, how the pieces fit, and what is actually worth using.

Salesforce started as a single CRM in 1999. Since roughly 2010 it has bought its way into nearly every adjacent category, marketing, commerce, integration, analytics, collaboration, data, and now AI agents, to become a full customer platform rather than a system of record. Most of the products you hear named are acquisitions that were renamed and folded toward the core.

That scale is exactly why it is hard to navigate. The same product has been renamed five or six times. A capability you need might be native and excellent, or a recent bolt-on that is still being woven in, or a long promise that has historically been hard to deliver. This tool keeps a consistent, honest signal on every product so a sales or marketing leader can decide what to lean on and what to scrutinize.

At Dreamforce in October 2025, Salesforce did something bigger than another product launch. It started retiring the word "Cloud." Sales Cloud became Agentforce Sales, Service Cloud became Agentforce Service, Data Cloud became Data 360, and the whole thing was wrapped as Agentforce 360. Marc Benioff has openly mused that the company itself might one day be called Agentforce. The pitch is a move from software you click through to autonomous agents that act on your behalf.

For an architect, the practical takeaway is this. Salesforce now wants to be the orchestration and agent layer over your data, and it is willing to sit on top of a warehouse it does not own through zero-copy. That changes the build-versus-buy math, especially if your real data foundation already lives in Snowflake or Databricks.

What "headless" actually means here

Headless means separating the content and logic from where it is rendered, so the same approved building blocks can be assembled into a web page, an email, a mobile screen, or an in-app message on demand. Salesforce branded this Headless 360 and is buying Contentful, a composable content platform, to supply the building blocks. The story they tell: Data 360 provides the context, Agentforce decides what to say, and Contentful holds the sanctioned content the agent assembles into a one-to-one experience.

The architect's reality check

This vision spans at least three pending or newly acquired pieces (Contentful, Fin, parts of Informatica) plus a rebrand that is still settling. The promise is coherent. The integration is not yet proven at the depth the keynote implies. If your data foundation already lives in a warehouse, the most durable pattern is to let that stay the source of truth and treat Data 360 plus Agentforce as an activation and orchestration layer via zero-copy, rather than re-platforming your data into Salesforce.
